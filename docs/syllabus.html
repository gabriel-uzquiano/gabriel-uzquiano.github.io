Course description

Modal logic extends the language of propositional and predicate logic with a sentential operator designed to capture the logic of necessity. This course will cover basic elements of propositional and quantified modal logic and selected philosophical applications, which may include the logic of belief and knowledge, tense logic, and the logic of preference and obligation. The semester will be divided into three main parts:

  1. Propositional Modal Logic

    We will cover elements of propositional logic: axiomatic presentations of propositional modal logic; a minimal modal logic ; axioms , , , and ; and metaphysical modality; possible worlds model theory; Kripke models; completeness and decidability results for a variety of propositional modal logics.

  2. Applications

    We will discuss some variations and philosophical applications of modal logic and the possible worlds model theory. They may include the logic of belief and knowledge, tense logic and the logic of conditionals. We will look at the distinction between material, indicative, and counterfactual conditionals; Robert Stalnaker’s logic of conditionals; and David Lewis’ logic of counterfactual conditionals.

  3. Quantified Modal Logic

    We will cover the distinction between de re and de dicto modal claims; the simplest quantified modal logic; the contrast between alternative possible worlds models for the language of quantified modal logic; and the question of whether existence is ever contingent or temporary.

  2. Further Resources

    There is no required textbook for the course, but you may find it helpful to look at the relevant sections of the open-access textbook:

    • Open Logic Project, Boxes and Diamonds

    One helpful resource for background in logic and set theory is the open-access book:

    • Open Logic Project, Open Logic Text

    Three more recommendations for textbooks in modal logic:

    • Burgess, John (2009) Philosophical Logic, Princeton University Press. Available online from USC Libraries.
    • Hughes, G. E. and Cresswell, H. (1996) A New Introduction to Modal Logic, Routledge. Available online from USC Libraries
    • van Benthem, J. (2010) Modal Logic for Open Minds. CSLI Publications.

    There are several relevant entries in the Stanford Encyclopedia you may find helpful, e.g., Modal Logic, Modern Origins of Modal Logic, Deontic Logic, Epistemic Logic, Intuitionistic Logic, and Temporal Logic.
